**Parametric Mechanical CAD with FreeCAD** teaches engineering design methodology by building **complete, functional mechanical mechanisms** with dimensions derived from kinematic analysis, manufacturing constraints, and engineering requirements, not arbitrary values or aesthetic preference.

Rather than learning FreeCAD tools in isolation, each lesson is built around **one fundamental mechanical mechanism** taken from real-world engineering practice. You learn **why** each dimension exists (kinematics, strength, manufacturability), **how** to implement it in CAD (sketching, constraints, assemblies), and **what** engineering decisions justify it (calculations, standards, analysis). No prior CAD experience required, but expect to think like an engineer from day one.

## Lessons

<Steps>
1. **[Slider Crank Mechanism](./slider-crank-mechanism)**
   The most common motion-conversion mechanism, used in engines, compressors, and pumps. Learn FreeCAD interface, sketching, constraints, part design, parameter tables, basic assemblies, and technical drawings.

2. **[Four-Bar Linkage Mechanism](./four-bar-linkage-mechanism)**
   Fundamental to hinges, folding systems, and mechanical linkages. Master construction geometry, master-sketch driven design, spreadsheet parameter control, and pin-jointed assemblies.

3. **[Scissor Lift Mechanism](./scissor-lift-mechanism)**
   Used in lifts, adjustable platforms, and deployable structures. Build repeating parametric geometry, manage symmetry, control stage count via parameters, and understand motion amplification.

4. **[Toggle Clamp Mechanism](./toggle-clamp-mechanism)**
   Widely used in manufacturing fixtures for locking and force amplification. Design with angle-based constraints, clearance-aware geometry, motion limits, and over-center locking behavior.

5. **[Pantograph Mechanism](./pantograph-mechanism)**
   Scales motion in copying and tracing mechanisms. Implement ratio-driven parameter relationships, similar triangle geometry, and expression-linked dimensions across assemblies.

6. **[Cam and Follower Mechanism](./cam-and-follower-mechanism)**
   Fundamental to timed motion in engines and automated machinery. Master curve-based sketching, datum axes, parametric cam profile control, and motion-defining geometry documentation.

7. **[Geneva Mechanism](./geneva-mechanism)**
   Provides precise intermittent motion and indexing. Design with polar geometry, pattern-based features, angular constraints, and locking geometry for intermittent motion control.

8. **[Scotch Yoke Mechanism](./scotch-yoke-mechanism)**
   Offers compact rotation-to-linear conversion with pure sinusoidal characteristics. Create slot-driven motion geometry, clearance considerations, and stroke-based parameterization.

9. **[Python Scripting for Advanced CAD](./python-scripting-advanced-cad)**
   Unlock FreeCAD's computational power with Python scripting. Generate mathematically-perfect gears, create lattice structures, automate design optimization, and perform batch operations impossible with GUI alone. Learn algorithmic design for research and advanced engineering.
</Steps>

### CAD Is Engineering Design Documentation, Not 3D Art

<Card title="‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Distinction" icon="warning">

**Computer-Aided Design is not computer-aided drawing.** Creating a 3D object on a computer is trivial. Engineering design demands that **every dimension, feature, and material choice has a defensible engineering rationale.**

A crank radius is not "50mm because it looks good." It is 50mm because:
- **Kinematic analysis** showed this stroke length meets force-displacement requirements
- **Stress calculations** confirmed the shaft can handle the torque at this radius
- **Manufacturing constraints** allow standard bearings at this size
- **Cost analysis** showed optimal material usage at this dimension
- **Assembly clearances** were verified against adjacent components

**Similarly:** Replicating someone else's CAD model on your computer is valuable CAD software practice, but it is not engineering design until you understand *why* each dimension exists. Creating visually sophisticated 3D models demonstrates software proficiency, not engineering competence. Engineering CAD requires you to derive dimensions from requirements, not copy them from examples or choose them for appearance.

</Card>

:::note[What Separates Engineering CAD from 3D Modeling]
**3D Modeling (Blender, Maya):**
- Dimensions chosen for visual aesthetics
- "Does it look right?" is the validation criterion
- Focus on appearance, rendering, animation

**Engineering CAD (FreeCAD, SolidWorks):**
- Dimensions derived from calculations, standards, or constraints
- "Does it function correctly and can it be manufactured?" is the validation criterion
- Focus on performance, tolerances, assemblies, manufacturing
:::

**Every dimension in this course exists for a reason:**
- **Crank radius** ‚Üí determines stroke length and mechanical advantage (kinematics)
- **Rod length-to-crank ratio** ‚Üí affects side forces and motion smoothness (dynamics)
- **Pin diameters** ‚Üí calculated from shear stress and bearing pressure (strength of materials)
- **Clearances** ‚Üí based on tolerance stack-up analysis (manufacturing)
- **Material selection** ‚Üí driven by stress, fatigue, and cost requirements (materials science)
- **Hole patterns** ‚Üí follow fastener standards and accessibility (assembly/maintenance)

:::tip[Engineering Design Process]
Before creating any CAD geometry, engineers ask:
1. **Functional requirements** - What must this mechanism do? (forces, motion, speed)
2. **Constraints** - What limits exist? (space, weight, cost, manufacturing capability)
3. **Analysis** - What math/physics governs this? (kinematics, strength, thermal, fluid)
4. **Standards compliance** - What codes/regulations apply? (safety, industry standards)
5. **Manufacturability** - How will this be made? (tolerances, processes, tooling access)
6. **Lifecycle** - How is it assembled, maintained, replaced? (ergonomics, serviceability)

Only after answering these questions do you open CAD software. **CAD documents engineering decisions; it does not make them.**
:::

:::note[Experience and Engineering Judgment]
With accumulated experience and domain expertise, engineers develop the ability to make informed estimates without detailed calculations for every dimension. A senior mechanical engineer might specify a shaft diameter based on pattern recognition from dozens of similar designs, material familiarity, and intuitive understanding of stress distributions.

**This is fundamentally different from arbitrary choice** because it is grounded in internalized engineering knowledge, industry standards, and prior analysis. The engineer could still justify the decision if challenged ("similar successful designs," "within safe stress limits for this material," "standard bearing size availability"). The estimate is defensible, traceable to engineering principles, and rooted in experience.

As a learner, you must first understand *why* before you can reliably estimate. This course builds that foundation.
:::

This course teaches you to think like an engineer using CAD as the documentation tool, not like an artist using CAD as a 3D canvas. You will justify dimensions with math, verify assemblies with motion studies, and generate drawings that communicate manufacturing intent.

## Why FreeCAD Over Alternatives?

This course uses **FreeCAD** instead of commercial CAD (Fusion 360, SolidWorks, Inventor) or free 3D software (Blender) for evidence-based reasons:

### ‚úÖ **Parametric Engineering CAD, Not Mesh Modeling**

**FreeCAD:** True parametric constraint-based CAD for mechanical engineering
- Designs defined by dimensions, constraints, and relationships
- Change one parameter ‚Üí entire design updates correctly
- Engineering intent preserved (e.g., "holes always concentric," "wall thickness constant")

**Blender:** Free but mesh-based modeling for animation/visual effects
- Not parametric‚Äîmoving vertices manually
- No constraint solver or dimensional control
- Excellent for art/rendering, wrong tool for mechanical engineering

**Commercial CAD (SolidWorks, Fusion 360):** Parametric, but costly and restrictive (see below)

### ‚úÖ **Engineering Capabilities**

- **Assembly Modeling:** Multi-part assemblies with motion constraints

**Educational advantages:**

### ‚ö†Ô∏è **Honest Limitations**

FreeCAD is **actively developed** but less mature than decades-old commercial CAD (SolidWorks since 1995, Inventor since 1999). Some manufacturers' workflows and machines integrate better with commercial CAD file formats and ecosystems. **However:** FreeCAD's STEP/IGES/STL export and standard engineering drawings are sufficient for manufacturing most designs you'll ever build, **provided you understand manufacturers' engineering requirements and communicate tolerances, materials, and processes clearly.** The limitation is rarely the software, but understanding manufacturing constraints.

**This course teaches engineering CAD methodology and manufacturing-aware design, not necessarily vendor-specific buttons.**
