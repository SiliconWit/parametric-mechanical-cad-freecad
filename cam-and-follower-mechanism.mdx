## üîß Engineering Context: Why This Mechanism Matters

<InArticleAd />

<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
Cam and follower mechanisms convert continuous rotary motion into precisely controlled reciprocating or oscillating motion. They're fundamental to timing and sequencing in mechanical systems, with the cam profile acting as a geometric program that defines the follower's motion.
</BionicText>

### Real-World Applications

Cam and follower mechanisms power critical timing and sequencing operations across industries:

:::note[Common Applications]
- **Engine valve trains** - Opening and closing valves at precise times during the combustion cycle
- **Automated assembly machines** - Programmed pick-and-place motion sequences
- **CNC machine tool changers** - Coordinated tool positioning and clamping
- **Textile machinery** - Precise needle control and fabric feeding
- **Printing presses** - Synchronized paper feed timing
- **Packaging equipment** - Coordinated cutting, folding, and sealing operations
:::

### The Engineering Problem

> **Design Challenge:** Given continuous rotary input motion, how do we create a specific, repeatable output motion profile with precise timing, smooth acceleration, and reliable contact?

**Key Concept:** The cam profile = motion program encoded as geometry

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Motion Programming" icon="star">
    The cam surface directly determines follower motion. As the cam rotates, its profile shape pushes the follower through a prescribed path.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Design Flexibility" icon="setting">

## üìö Mechanism Fundamentals

<InArticleAd />


### Components and Motion

A cam and follower system consists of three essential elements:

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Components">

**1. Cam: Rotating Driver**
- Plate cam (disk) with profiled edge
- Rotates continuously at constant speed
- Profile shape programs the motion

**2. Follower: Moving Output**
- Translates or oscillates
- Maintains contact with cam surface
- Executes programmed motion

**3. Frame: Fixed Reference**
- Provides cam shaft bearing
- Guides follower motion
- Maintains alignment

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Follower Types">

<Card title="‚öôÔ∏è Follower Configurations" icon="puzzle">

**Roller Follower (Most Common)**
- Rolling contact reduces friction
- Suitable for high-speed operation
- Used in this lesson

**Flat-Faced Follower**
- Flat contact surface
- Simple geometry
- Good for low-speed applications

**Knife-Edge Follower**
- Point contact (theoretical)
- Impractical due to high wear
- Used in analysis only

</Card>

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Motion Profile">

**Standard Cam Cycle:**

A complete 360¬∞ rotation typically includes four phases:

**Rise: Outward Motion**
- Follower moves away from cam center
- Lift distance: L
- Rise angle: typically 90-120¬∞

**Dwell: High Position**
- Follower remains stationary at maximum lift
- Cam continues rotating
- Constant radius circular arc

**Return: Inward Motion**
- Follower moves back toward cam center
- Return angle: typically 90-120¬∞

**Dwell: Low Position**
- Follower remains at base position
- Ready for next cycle

:::tip[Engineering Insight]
The rise and return sections require careful motion law selection. Sudden velocity changes cause vibration and wear. Professional cam design uses harmonic, cycloidal, or polynomial motion curves to ensure smooth acceleration profiles.
:::

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Key Parameters

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Geometric Parameters">

**Base Circle Radius (R_b)**
- Minimum cam radius
- Determines minimum follower position
- Foundation of cam geometry

**Lift (L)**
- Maximum follower displacement
- Distance from base to peak position
- Critical design parameter

**Prime Circle Radius (R_p)**
- R_p = R_b + L
- Maximum cam radius
- Defines peak follower position

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Timing Parameters">

**Rise Angle**
- Angular duration of outward motion
- Typical: 90-120¬∞
- Affects acceleration

**Dwell Angles**
- High dwell: follower at maximum lift
- Low dwell: follower at base position
- Total angles must sum to 360¬∞

**Return Angle**
- Angular duration of inward motion
- Often mirrors rise angle
- Affects deceleration

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Motion Laws">

**Simple Harmonic Motion**

- Smooth acceleration
- Good for moderate speeds
- Used in this lesson

$$
s(\theta) = \frac{L}{2} \left[ 1 - \cos\left( \frac{\pi \theta}{\theta_{\text{rise}}} \right) \right]
$$

Where:
- $$ s(\theta) $$ is the follower displacement
- $$ \theta $$ is the cam rotation angle
- $$ L $$ is the total lift
- $$ \theta_{\text{rise}} $$ is the cam angle over which the rise occurs

**Cycloidal Motion**
- Smoother acceleration than harmonic
- Ideal for high-speed cams
- More complex mathematics

**Polynomial Motion**
- Customizable acceleration profiles
- Advanced applications
- Requires numerical calculation

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

## üí° Part 2: Design Strategy

<InArticleAd />


<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
Cam design requires thinking in terms of motion profiles first, then translating those profiles into geometry. The cam surface is literally a physical encoding of the desired motion: every point on the profile corresponds to a specific follower position at a specific rotation angle.
</BionicText>

### Our Motion Profile

<Card title="üéØ Cam Cycle Specification" icon="rocket">

**360¬∞ Complete Cycle:**

- **0¬∞ to 120¬∞:** Rise (follower lifts 20mm using simple harmonic motion)
- **120¬∞ to 180¬∞:** Dwell (follower remains at maximum lift)
- **180¬∞ to 300¬∞:** Return (follower descends 20mm using simple harmonic motion)
- **300¬∞ to 360¬∞:** Dwell (follower remains at base position)

This creates smooth, repeatable motion with predictable timing.

</Card>

### Design Parameters

We'll control the entire mechanism with these key parameters:

```
BaseCircleRadius = 40 mm
Lift = 20 mm
RollerRadius = 10 mm
CamThickness = 15 mm
ShaftDiameter = 20 mm
```

:::note[Why These Values?]
- **BaseCircleRadius = 40mm:** Large enough for structural integrity, small enough for compact design
- **Lift = 20mm:** Sufficient motion range for demonstration
- **RollerRadius = 10mm:** Provides rolling contact while fitting within motion range
- **Cam thickness and shaft:** Standard proportions for rigid, machinable components
:::

## üîß Part 4: Designing the Cam Profile

<InArticleAd />


### Understanding Cam Profile Design

<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
The cam profile must be designed relative to the follower position. For a roller follower, we actually design the pitch curve: the path traced by the roller center. Then the actual cam surface is offset inward by the roller radius. This ensures the roller maintains proper contact throughout the cycle.
</BionicText>

### Design Approach

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Motion Mathematics">

**Simple Harmonic Motion Rise:**

For smooth acceleration during rise, we use simple harmonic motion:

$$
s(\theta) = L \times \left[1 - \cos\left(\frac{\pi \times \theta}{\theta_{\text{rise}}}\right)\right] / 2
$$

**Where:**
- $s$ = follower displacement from base position
- $\theta$ = current cam angle from start of rise
- $\theta_{\text{rise}}$ = total rise angle (120¬∞)
- $L$ = total lift (20mm)

**Cam Radius at Angle Œ∏:**

$$
R(\theta) = R_b + s(\theta)
$$

**Where:**
- $R_b$ = base circle radius

**Key Points for 120¬∞ Rise:**

| Angle | Displacement | Radius |
|-------|--------------|--------|
| 0¬∞ | 0 mm | 40 mm |
| 30¬∞ | 2.68 mm | 42.68 mm |
| 60¬∞ | 10 mm | 50 mm |
| 90¬∞ | 17.32 mm | 57.32 mm |
| 120¬∞ | 20 mm | 60 mm |

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Sketching Strategy">

**Two Approaches:**

**Option A: B-Spline Through Calculated Points** (Accurate)
- Calculate exact positions using motion law formulas
- Place points at key angles
- Connect with smooth B-spline curve
- Most accurate representation

**Option B: Circular Arc Approximation** (Simplified)
- Use circular arcs to approximate smooth curves
- Easier to construct and constrain
- Acceptable for demonstration and low-speed applications
- We'll use this approach for simplicity

:::note[Production vs. Learning]
Professional cam design often uses specialized software or Python scripts to generate exact profiles from motion equations. For learning FreeCAD fundamentals, we'll use the approximation method to focus on CAD techniques rather than mathematical complexity.
:::

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Construction Plan">

**Cam Profile Construction:**

**Base Circle:**
- Circle centered at origin
- Radius = BaseCircleRadius (40mm)
- Represents dwell at base position

**Rise Section (0¬∞ to 120¬∞):**
- Smooth curve from 40mm to 60mm radius
- Approximate with arc or B-spline

**High Dwell (120¬∞ to 180¬∞):**
- Circular arc at PrimeCircleRadius (60mm)
- Constant radius maintains follower at max lift

**Return Section (180¬∞ to 300¬∞):**
- Mirror of rise section
- Smooth curve from 60mm back to 40mm

**Low Dwell (300¬∞ to 360¬∞):**
- Circular arc at BaseCircleRadius (40mm)
- Completes the cycle

**Shaft Hole:**
- Circle at origin
- Radius = ShaftRadius (10mm)

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Drawing the Rise Section

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Construction Lines">

We'll use construction geometry to establish angular positions:

<Steps>

1. **Draw horizontal reference line**

   - **Line tool** (press L)
   - Draw from origin horizontally to the right
   - Press **Escape**

2. **Make it construction geometry**

   - Select the line
   - Press **G** key (toggles construction mode)
   - Line turns blue (construction)

3. **Constrain it horizontal**

   - Select the line
   - Press **H** key
   - Now it's locked horizontal

4. **Set the length**

   - **Distance constraint** on line length
   - Type: `70` mm (extends beyond prime circle)

</Steps>

This horizontal line at 0¬∞ is our angular reference.

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Rise Arc (Simplified)">

**Simplified approach using a single arc:**

<Steps>

1. **Draw arc from base to prime circle**

   - **Arc tool** (or press A)
   - Click at angle 0¬∞, radius 40mm (on base circle, at horizontal line)
   - Click at angle 120¬∞, radius 60mm (estimate position)
   - Click a third point to define arc curvature
   - Press **Escape**

2. **Constrain start point**

   - **Coincident constraint**
   - Arc start point ‚Üí Base circle
   - Arc start point ‚Üí Horizontal reference line
   - This locks it at 0¬∞, 40mm radius

3. **Constrain end point angle**

   - Draw construction line from origin at 120¬∞ angle
   - **Angle constraint:** 120¬∞ relative to horizontal
   - **Coincident:** Arc end point ‚Üí this 120¬∞ line

4. **Constrain end point radius**

   - **Distance constraint** from origin to arc endpoint
   - Click **∆íx** button
   - Type: `Spreadsheet.PrimeCircleRadius`
   - Press **Enter**

</Steps>

The rise section is now constrained!

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Rise Spline (Advanced)">

**For more accurate harmonic motion (optional):**

<Steps>

1. **Calculate and place points**

   Using the table from earlier:
   - Point 1: 0¬∞, 40mm radius
   - Point 2: 30¬∞, 42.68mm radius
   - Point 3: 60¬∞, 50mm radius
   - Point 4: 90¬∞, 57.32mm radius
   - Point 5: 120¬∞, 60mm radius

2. **Use Point tool**

   Place points at each calculated position

3. **Constrain each point**

   - Distance from origin (radius)
   - Angle from horizontal (use construction lines)

4. **Connect with B-spline**

   - **B-spline tool** (press S)
   - Click each point in sequence
   - Press **Escape** when complete

</Steps>

This creates a smooth curve matching the harmonic motion law exactly.

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Steps>

### Drawing the Return Section

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Mirror Approach">

<Steps>

3. **Apply symmetry constraint**

   - Select rise geometry

4. **Position correctly**

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Manual Construction">

**Alternative: Draw return section manually**

<Steps>

1. **Draw return arc**

   - Arc from 180¬∞ position (60mm radius) to 300¬∞ position (40mm radius)

2. **Constrain endpoints**

   - Start: Coincident to high dwell end
   - End: Use construction line at 300¬∞ angle

3. **Constrain radii**

</Steps>

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Drawing the Low Dwell

<Steps>

1. **Draw arc at base circle radius**

   - **Arc tool**
   - Center at **origin**
   - Start at 300¬∞ (end of return)
   - End at 360¬∞ (0¬∞, completing the cycle)

2. **Constrain the arc**

   - **Radius constraint** ‚Üí Click **∆íx** ‚Üí `Spreadsheet.BaseCircleRadius`
   - **Coincident:** Arc start ‚Üí Return section end

</Steps>

The profile is now a complete closed curve!

### Adding the Shaft Hole

<Steps>

1. **Draw center hole**

   - **Circle tool**
   - Click at **origin**
   - Draw small circle
   - Press **Escape**

2. **Constrain the hole**

   - **Coincident:** Circle center ‚Üí Origin

<Steps>

   - Should say **"Fully constrained"** (or close to it)

2. **Visual verification**

   - Circular top section (120¬∞ to 180¬∞)

</Steps>

## üéØ Part 5: Creating the Follower System

<InArticleAd />


### Follower Design Intent

<Card title="üîß Follower Assembly Components" icon="puzzle">

The follower system consists of three parts:

**Roller**
- Cylindrical rolling element
- Maintains contact with cam surface
- Reduces friction

**Follower Stem**
- Rectangular shaft
- Translates vertically
- Transfers motion to load

**Guide Mount** (part of frame)
- Constrains lateral motion
- Allows only vertical translation

</Card>

### Creating the Roller

<Steps>

1. **Create new Body**

   - Part Design workbench
   - Create Body
   - Rename: `Roller`

2. **Create Sketch on XZ_Plane**

   Why XZ? The follower moves vertically (Z-axis) and we'll revolve around a horizontal axis

   - Select Roller body
   - Create Sketch ‚Üí **XZ_Plane**

3. **Draw roller profile**

   - **Circle tool**
   - Center position: X = 0, Z = 70 (above cam - we'll position precisely in assembly)
   - Click to place circle
   - Press **Escape**

4. **Constrain the circle**

   - **Horizontal constraint:** Circle center ‚Üí Origin (to center it on rotation axis)
   - **Distance constraint:** Circle center to origin in Z direction: `70` mm
   - **Radius constraint** ‚Üí Click **∆íx** ‚Üí `Spreadsheet.RollerRadius`

5. **Check and close sketch**

   - Solver: Fully constrained
   - Close

</Steps>

### Creating 3D Roller with Revolve

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="What is Revolve?">

Perfect for:
- Cylinders
- Shafts
- Pulleys
- Wheels
- Any rotationally symmetric part

</Card>

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Applying Revolve">

<Steps>

1. **Select the roller sketch** in the tree

2. **Click Revolve tool** in Part Design toolbar

3. **In the Revolve panel:**
   - Axis: Select **Vertical** or **Z-axis**
   - Angle: `360¬∞` (full rotation)

</Steps>

<Steps>

   - OK

## üèóÔ∏è Part 6: Creating the Base Frame

<InArticleAd />


### Frame Design Intent

The frame provides:
- **Cam shaft bearing** - Fixed pivot for cam rotation
- **Follower guide** - Constrains follower to vertical motion only
- **Structural base** - Rigid reference for entire mechanism

### Creating the Frame

<Steps>

1. **Create new Body**

   - Create Body ‚Üí Rename: `Frame`

2. **Create Sketch on XY_Plane**

3. **Draw base plate**

   - **Rectangle tool**
   - Width: 150mm
   - Height: 100mm
   - Centered at origin

   - **Symmetric constraints:** Center rectangle about both X and Y axes
   - **Distance constraints:** Width = `150` mm, Height = `100` mm

4. **Add cam shaft hole**

   - **Circle tool** at origin
   - **Coincident:** Circle center ‚Üí Origin
   - **Radius constraint** ‚Üí `Spreadsheet.ShaftRadius + 0.5` (add clearance)

   Note: You can type the formula directly or use a fixed value like `10.5` mm

5. **Add follower guide slot**

   - Draw two vertical parallel lines for guide rails
   - Position: X = ¬±10 mm (creates 20mm wide slot for 15mm stem + clearance)
   - Height: 100mm (full range of motion)

   - **Symmetric:** Left and right lines about Y-axis
   - **Distance between:** `20` mm

6. **Close sketch**

7. **Pad the frame**

   - Pad tool
   - Length: `20` mm
   - OK

</Steps>

**Frame complete!**

## üß© Part 7: Assembly

<InArticleAd />


<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
Assembly transforms individual parts into a functioning mechanism. For the cam and follower, we must establish rotational motion for the cam while constraining the follower to pure vertical translation. The roller maintains contact with the cam surface through careful positioning.
</BionicText>

### Assembly Strategy

<Card title="üéØ Assembly Constraints Plan" icon="rocket">

1. **Frame:** Fixed (ground link)
2. **Cam:** Rotates about Z-axis through shaft hole
3. **Follower Stem:** Constrained to vertical (Z-direction) translation only
4. **Roller:** Positioned at bottom of stem, contacts cam surface

</Card>

### Creating the Assembly

<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Setup">

<Steps>

1. **Switch to Assembly workbench**

   Use workbench dropdown (may be labeled "Assembly 3" or "Assembly 4" depending on version)

2. **Create new assembly**

   Assembly ‚Üí Create Assembly

   An assembly container appears in the tree

3. **Add parts**

   Drag parts from tree into assembly or use "Add Part" button:
   - Frame
   - Cam
   - FollowerStem
   - Roller

</Steps>

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Fixing Frame">

<Steps>

1. **Select Frame** in assembly tree

2. **Apply Fixed constraint**

   Click **Fixed** or **Lock** constraint button

3. **Frame is now locked in place**

   This serves as the ground reference for all motion

</Steps>

:::note[Why Fix the Frame?]
Every mechanism needs a fixed reference point. The frame provides this ground link from which all other motion is measured and controlled.
:::

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Cam Rotation">

**Allow cam to rotate about shaft:**

<Steps>

1. **Select cam shaft hole axis**

   Click on the circular edge or use the References panel

2. **Select frame shaft hole axis**

3. **Apply Axial Align constraint**

   This aligns the axes and allows rotation about that axis

</Steps>

**Test:** Try rotating the cam manually in the assembly view - it should spin about the Z-axis!

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Follower Translation">

**Constrain follower to vertical motion only:**

<Steps>

1. **Select follower stem body**

2. **Select frame guide surfaces**

   You may need to select the parallel guide rails or frame top surface

3. **Apply constraints for vertical sliding:**

   - **Plane constraint:** Aligns stem face to frame reference
   - **Linear motion constraint:** Allows only Z-direction movement

   The exact constraint type depends on your Assembly workbench version

</Steps>

**Goal:** Follower can slide up and down (Z-axis) but cannot move sideways or rotate

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Roller Position">

**Position roller at bottom of stem:**

<Steps>

1. **Select roller** and **follower stem**

2. **Apply constraints:**

   - **Coincident** or **Attachment:** Position roller at stem bottom
   - Align axes if needed

3. **Adjust height** so roller just touches cam base circle

</Steps>

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Testing Motion">

**Manual motion verification:**

<Steps>

1. **Rotate cam slowly** in the assembly

   Grab the cam and rotate it incrementally

2. **Manually adjust follower height** to maintain roller contact with cam

   As cam rotates through rise, move follower up
   During dwell, keep follower stationary
   During return, move follower down

3. **Observe the cycle:**

   - 0¬∞ to 120¬∞: Follower rises
   - 120¬∞ to 180¬∞: Follower stays high (dwell)
   - 180¬∞ to 300¬∞: Follower descends
   - 300¬∞ to 360¬∞: Follower stays low (dwell)

</Steps>

:::tip[Assembly Workbench Limitations]
FreeCAD's Assembly workbench doesn't automatically simulate continuous contact between cam and follower. For full motion simulation, you would need:
- Dedicated cam simulation software
- FreeCAD plugins for contact analysis
- Export to dynamics software (e.g., Adams, RecurDyn)

For learning and design verification, manual positioning demonstrates the motion principle.
:::

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

## ‚ùì Common Issues and Solutions

<InArticleAd />


<Tabs>
<TabItem label="Profile Issues">

**"Cam profile has sharp corners"**

:::caution[Solution]
- **Cause:** Abrupt transitions between rise and dwell sections
- **Fix:** Add tangent constraints where sections meet
- **Better:** Use B-spline that smoothly blends into circular arcs
- **Best:** Calculate transition regions using polynomial blends
:::

**"Profile doesn't close properly"**

:::caution[Solution]
- **Check:** End of low dwell must connect to start of rise
- **Use Coincident constraint** on start/end points
- **Verify:** Angular constraints sum to 360¬∞
:::

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Motion Issues">

**"Follower motion is jerky"**

:::caution[Solution]
- **Cause:** Rise section not smooth (straight lines or sharp arcs)
- **Fix:** Use smooth curves (B-spline or multiple blended arcs)
- **Theory:** Acceleration should be continuous (no jumps)
- **Reference:** Simple harmonic or cycloidal motion laws
:::

**"Roller doesn't maintain contact"**

:::caution[Solution]
- In real mechanism: Add return spring
- In CAD assembly: Manually position for each cam angle
- For simulation: Need dedicated cam analysis software
:::

</TabItem>
<TabItem label="Parametric Issues">

**"Cam profile breaks when changing parameters"**

:::caution[Solution]
- **Check:** Are rise/return sections using formulas?
- **Fix:** Link critical radii to `Spreadsheet.BaseCircleRadius` and `Spreadsheet.PrimeCircleRadius`
- **Verify:** Construction geometry (angular lines) properly constrained?
- **Test:** Change parameters in small increments to identify which constraint breaks
:::

**"Cannot create B-spline smoothly"**

:::caution[Solution]
- **Alternative:** Use circular arc approximation (3-5 arcs)
- **Tip:** For 120¬∞ rise, try arcs at 40¬∞, 80¬∞, 120¬∞ with blended radii
- **Advanced:** Use Python script to generate exact points from motion equation
:::

</TabItem>
</Tabs>
