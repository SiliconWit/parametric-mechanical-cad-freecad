<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
The slider crank mechanism is one of the most fundamental and widely used motion conversion mechanisms in mechanical engineering. It converts continuous rotational motion into reciprocating linear motion (or vice versa), powering everything from internal combustion engines to reciprocating compressors and pumps.
</BionicText>

### Real-World Applications

The slider crank appears everywhere in engineering:

:::note[Common Applications]
- **Internal combustion engines** - Piston-crankshaft system converting combustion force to rotation
- **Reciprocating compressors** - Converting motor rotation to piston compression cycles
- **Reciprocating pumps** - Fluid pumping through linear piston motion
- **Sewing machines** - Converting motor rotation to needle reciprocation
- **Hand-operated water pumps** - Manual rotation creating pumping action
:::

### The Engineering Problem

> **Design Challenge:** Given a rotating crankshaft, how do we create linear reciprocating motion with controlled stroke length and predictable motion characteristics?

:::tip[Why Slider Cranks Are Engineering Workhorses]
**Key Advantages:**
- **Simple and reliable** - Only three moving parts plus frame
- **Predictable motion** - Well-understood kinematics and dynamics
- **Scalable design** - Works from tiny medical devices to massive ship engines
- **Efficient power transmission** - Direct conversion of rotary to linear motion
- **Easy to manufacture** - Standard parts and conventional machining

**Design Trade-offs:**
- Side forces on slider guides (requires robust guide design)
- Non-sinusoidal motion profile (differs slightly from pure harmonic motion)
- Connecting rod length affects motion characteristics (l/r ratio critical)
:::

## üìö Mechanism Fundamentals


<InArticleAd />

### Components and Motion

A slider crank consists of four elements working together:

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Components">

    **1. Crank (Link 2)**
    - Rotates about fixed pivot (crankshaft)
    - Radius r determines stroke length

    **2. Connecting Rod (Link 3)**
    - Transmits motion between crank and slider
    - Length l affects motion profile

    **3. Slider (Link 4)**
    - Moves linearly along fixed path
    - Provides reciprocating output

    **4. Frame (Link 1)**
    - Fixed reference
    - Provides pivot and guide

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Key Parameters">

    <Card title="‚öôÔ∏è Critical Design Parameters" icon="setting">

    **Crank radius (r)**
    - Distance from crank pivot to rod connection
    - Directly determines stroke: S = 2r

    **Connecting rod length (l)**
    - Distance between rod pivots
    - Affects motion profile and side forces

    **Length ratio (l/r)**
    - Typical range: l/r ‚â• 3
    - Higher ratio = more sinusoidal motion
    - Lower ratio = higher side forces

    </Card>

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Motion Analysis">

    **Stroke Relationship:**

    $$S = 2r$$

    where $S$ is the stroke and $r$ is the crank radius.

    **Design Guidelines:**
    - l/r ‚â• 3 for minimal side forces
    - l/r = 4 to 5 common in engines
    - Shorter rods = more compact but higher loads

    :::tip[Engineering Insight]
    The connecting rod length ratio (l/r) is critical. Too short creates excessive side forces on the slider guides; too long makes the mechanism unnecessarily large. Engine designers carefully optimize this ratio for each application.
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## üî© Part 4: Creating the Crank


<InArticleAd />

### Design Intent

<Card title="‚öôÔ∏è Crank Requirements" icon="document">

The crank is a rotating disc with:

- **Central hole** for the fixed pivot (shaft)
- **Offset hole** for the connecting rod pin
- **Parametric control** - radius driven by `Spreadsheet.CrankRadius`

</Card>

### Step-by-Step: Crank Part

<Steps>

1. **Create a Body**

   - Switch to **Part Design workbench**
   - Click **Create Body** button (or Part Design ‚Üí Create body)
   - A "Body" object appears in the tree
   - Right-click ‚Üí Rename ‚Üí type `Crank`

2. **Create a Sketch**

   - Select the "Crank" body in tree
   - Click **Create Sketch** button (or Sketch ‚Üí Create Sketch)
   - Dialog asks: Choose a plane
   - Select **XY_Plane**

   You're now in **Sketcher workbench** (automatic switch)

</Steps>

### Understanding the Sketcher

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Sketcher Basics">

    The Sketcher is where you create 2D profiles that become 3D parts.

    **Key Concept: Constraints**

    A sketch is "fully constrained" when every dimension is defined‚Äîzero degrees of freedom (DOF). This is the goal!

    **Why full constraints matter:**
    - ‚úÖ Predictable behavior when parameters change
    - ‚úÖ No unexpected movement or sizing
    - ‚úÖ Professional parametric design

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Sketcher Tools">

    **Geometry Tools:**
    - Line (L)
    - Circle (C)
    - Arc
    - Rectangle
    - B-spline (S)

    **Constraint Tools:**
    - Coincident (joins points)
    - Horizontal / Vertical
    - Parallel / Perpendicular
    - Equal (equal lengths)
    - Symmetric
    - Distance
    - Radius
    - Angle

    **Check Status:**
    - Look at "Solver messages" panel
    - "Fully constrained" = perfect!
    - "X degrees of freedom" = need more constraints

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Pro Tips">

    :::tip[Sketcher Efficiency Tips]
    1. **Use keyboard shortcuts** - C for circle, L for line, Escape to exit tool
    2. **Select first, constrain second** - Click what you want to constrain, then apply constraint
    3. **Watch the solver messages** - It tells you what's missing
    4. **Use Ctrl+click** to select multiple items at once
    5. **Right-click for context menus** - Often faster than toolbar
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Drawing the Crank Profile

<Steps>

1. **Draw the outer circle**

   - Click **Circle tool drop down arrow** then click **Create Circle by Center** (or **press 'G' and then 'C' in quick succession**)
   - Click at the **origin** (look for white/yellow dot at center)
   - Move mouse outward and click to complete circle
   - Press **Escape**

2. **Draw the shaft hole**

   - **Circle tool** again
   - Click at **origin**
   - Draw a smaller circle inside
   - Press **Escape**

3. **Draw the rod pin hole**

   - **Circle tool** again
   - Click somewhere to the **right** of center, at the origin horizontal line
   - Draw a small circle
   - Press **Escape**

   You now have 3 circles, but they're not constrained properly yet!

</Steps>

### Adding Geometric Constraints

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Center Hole">

    **Make shaft hole concentric with origin (if you did not already *'Click at the origin'* above):**

    <Steps>
    1. Click **Coincident constraint** tool
    2. Click the **center** of the inner small circle
    3. Click the **origin point**
    4. Press **Escape**
    </Steps>

    The shaft hole is now locked to the center!

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Pin Alignment">

    **Make rod pin horizontally aligned (if you did not already *'Click at the origin horizontal line'* above):**

    <Steps>
    1. Click **Horizontal constraint** tool
    2. Click the **center** of the offset circle
    3. Click the **origin**
    4. Press **Escape**
    </Steps>

    Alternative shortcut:
    - Select both points (Ctrl+click)
    - Press **H** key

    The pin hole is now horizontally aligned!

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Why Geometric First?">

    :::note[Constraint Strategy]
    **Always apply geometric constraints before dimensions!**

    **Geometric** (Coincident, Horizontal, Parallel, etc.)
    - Define relationships and design intent
    - Lock down the configuration

    **Dimensional** (Distance, Radius, Angle)
    - Set the sizes
    - Should come after geometry is locked

    This order creates stable, predictable parametric behavior.
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Adding Dimensions (The Parametric Part!)

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Outer Radius">

    <Steps>
    1. Click the **outer circle**, then **press 'K' and 'R' in quick succession** to activate the **Radius Constraint Dimensioning Tool** (or go to Sketch ‚Üí Sketcher Constraints ‚Üí Constrain Radius).
    2. Type `40`
    3. Click **OK**.
    </Steps>

    Crank disk is now 40mm radius.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Shaft Hole">

    <Steps>
    1. Click the **inner circle**, then **press 'K' and 'R' in quick succession**
    2. Type: `8`
    3. Press **Enter**
    </Steps>

    8mm radius shaft hole created.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Rod Pin Hole">

    <Steps>
    1. Click the **offset circle**, then **press 'K' and 'R' in quick succession**
    2. Type: `5`
    3. Press **Enter**
    </Steps>

    5mm radius pin hole created.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Parametric Distance!">

    <Card title="üéØ Critical Parametric Link" icon="star">

    This is where it gets powerful!

    </Card>

    <Steps>
    1. Click the **origin point**, then click the **origin point of the Rod Pin Hole circle**.
    2. **Press 'L'** to activate the **Constraint Horizontal Distance Dimensioning Tool**. **Don't type a number!** 
    3. Click the small **fx** button and in the formula editor, start typing *Spreadsh...* and `<<Spreadsheet>>` will appear. Then, click it. Next, start typing the alias *Crank...* and `CrankRadius` will appear. Click it.
    4. Click **OK** and you will see `Radius: 30.00`.
    5. Click **OK** again.
    </Steps>

    **Magic!** The dimension now shows "30mm" and will update whenever you change the spreadsheet!

    :::tip[This Is Parametric Design]
    You just linked a dimension to a parameter. This single step is what separates amateur CAD from professional parametric design. The geometry is now **'intelligent'** and **controllable**.
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Finalizing the Sketch

<Steps>

1. **Check the solver**

   Look at "Solver Messages" panel (left side):
   - Should say **"Fully constrained"**
   - If it says "X degrees of freedom", you're missing constraints

2. **Close the sketch**

   Click the **Close** button in the toolbar

   The sketch is now a 2D profile visible in the 3D view

</Steps>

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Viewing Your Part">

    **Navigate the 3D view:**

    - See the disc with holes!

## üîó Part 5: Creating the Connecting Rod


<InArticleAd />

### Design Intent

The connecting rod is a rectangular plate with circular holes at each end for pins.

<Card title="üîß Rod Requirements" icon="puzzle">

- **Parametric length** - Controlled by `Spreadsheet.RodLength`
- **Holes at both ends** - For crank pin and slider pin
- **Rectangular body** - Structural beam between holes

</Card>

### Creating the Rod

<Steps>

1. **Create new Body**

   - Part Design workbench
   - Create Body
   - Rename: `ConnectingRod`
   - Select `Crank`, right-click, then choose **Hide Selection** to remove the crank obstruction from the next sketch/body

2. **Create Sketch**

   - Select ConnectingRod body
   - Create Sketch ‚Üí XY_Plane

</Steps>

### Drawing the Rod Profile 

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Outer Circles">

    **Create the end profiles:**

    <Steps>
    1. **Circle tool** (press **G, C**)
    2. Click **anywhere on the left side** of the sketch area and draw the first circle
    3. Repeat the process **to the right** of the first circle (arbitrary distance) to draw the second circle
    4. Press **Escape**

    5. Select the **left circle**
    6. Radius constraint: Press **K, R**
       - Type: `10` mm  
       - Enter

    7. **Repeat for right circle:**
       - **K, R** ‚Üí Radius = `8` mm
       - Enter
    </Steps>

    The two circles define the rod ends. Their spacing will be controlled later using constraints.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Top & Bottom Lines">

    **Create tangent boundaries:**

    <Steps>
    1. **Line tool** (press **G, L**)
    2. Draw a **long horizontal line above** both circles  
       - Start **well before** the left circle  
       - End **well after** the right circle
    3. Press **Escape**

    4. Draw a **second horizontal line below** both circles
       - Again, extend it past both circles
    5. Press **Escape**
    </Steps>

    These lines will later become tangent to the circles and trimmed to form the rod body.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Tangent Constraints & Trim">

    **Constrain and clean geometry:**

    <Steps>
    1. **Apply tangent constraints**:
       - Click **top line**, then **left circle arc**, press **T**
       - Repeat for:
         - Top line ‚Üí left circle
         - Top line ‚Üí right circle
         - Bottom line ‚Üí left circle
         - Bottom line ‚Üí right circle  
       *(4 tangents total)*

    2. **Trim excess geometry**:
       - Trim tool (**G, T**)
       - Trim the **overextended ends** of the top and bottom lines (4 trims)
       - Trim the **inner arcs** of both circles between the lines
    3. Press **Escape**
    </Steps>

    The rod outline is now defined by tangency ‚Äî strong, clean constraints with minimal dimensions.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Alignment & Length">

    **Align and parametrize the sketch:**

    <Steps>
    1. **Align circle centers horizontally**:
       - Click **left circle center**
       - Click **right circle center**
       - Press **H**

    2. **Set rod length parametrically**:
       - Click **left circle center**
       - Click **right circle center**
       - Press **L** (horizontal distance)
       - Click **∆íx**
       - Enter: `Spreadsheet.RodLength`
       - OK

    3. **Fix left end to origin**:
       - Click **left circle center**
       - Click **origin**
       - Press **C** (coincident)
    </Steps>

    The rod length is now fully driven by the spreadsheet and anchored to the origin.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Check & Pad">

    **Verify and create solid:**

    :::caution[Check Solver]
    Solver should report: **"Fully constrained"**

    If not:
    - Missing tangent constraint?
    - Circle centers not horizontally aligned?
    - Left circle not coincident with origin?
    :::

    <Steps>
    1. Confirm **Fully constrained**
    2. **Close sketch**
    3. **Pad** ‚Üí Length = `8` mm
    4. OK
    </Steps>

    The outer rod body is complete.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Inner Holes">

    **Create pin holes using a secondary sketch:**

    <Steps>
    1. Click the **top face** of the rod
    2. **Create sketch** (go to *Sketch* ‚Üí *Create Sketch*)

    3. **Left inner hole**:
       - **G, C**
       - Click the **origin**
       - **K, R** ‚Üí Radius = `8` mm
       - OK

    4. **Right inner hole**:
       - Enable **external geometry** (**G, X**)
       - Click the **right outer arc**
       - **G, C**
       - Click the arc center
       - **K, R** ‚Üí Radius = `6` mm
       - OK
    </Steps>

    Using external geometry guarantees perfect alignment with the outer profile.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Hole Feature & Finish">

    **Cut through the body:**

    <Steps>
    1. Close sketch
    2. Select **one hole circle sketch**
       - If hidden, click the **eye icon** or double-click the sketch
    3. Click **Hole tool**
       - Depth: **Through all**
       - Diameter: `R √ó 2`
       - OK

    4. **Repeat** for the second hole
    5. Press **V, F** ‚Üí View Fit
    6. **Save**: **Ctrl + S**
    </Steps>

    **Connecting rod complete and fully parametric!**

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## üì¶ Part 6: Creating the Slider


<InArticleAd />

### Design Intent

The slider is a block that moves linearly, with a hole for the connecting rod pin.

### Quick Creation Steps

<Steps>

1. Hide the `ConnectingRod` by clicking the eye
2. **Body:** go to *Part Design* ‚Üí *Create Body*, then rename to `Slider`

3. **Sketch on XY_Plane:**

   - **Sketch:** Click `Slider`, then go to *Sketch* ‚Üí *Create Sketch*, click **XY-Plane**
   - **Rectangle:** Press **G, R**, then click an arbitrary location to draw a rectangle, then **Escape** or **right click**
   - **Symmetric constraints:** Click **top right corner**, then **bottom left corner**, then **click origin**, then press **S** to center the rectangle about X and Y axes
   - **Length Dimension:** Click the **bottom left corner**, then the **bottom right corner**, then press **L**, then enter Length = `40` mm 
   - **Height Dimension:** Click the **bottom left corner**, then the **top left corner**, then press **I**, then enter Height = `30` mm

4. **Check:** Fully constrained, then **close** the sketch

5. **Pad:** Length = `20` mm

6. **Circle:** At origin for pin hole

   - Click the pad and select the top face
   - Go to *Sketch* ‚Üí *Create Sketch*
   - Press **G, C** to draw a circle of an arbitrary radius starting from the **origin** within the slider block, then **right click** or **Escape**
   - Click the **circle arc**, press **K, R**, then enter radius `10` mm (arbitrary within the block to achieve a fully constrained sketch), and **close** the sketch
   - Click the **Hole tool**, set depth to **Through all**, and diameter to be `12 mm`, then **OK**
   - Press **CRTL + S** to save your part design

</Steps>

**Slider complete!**

## üß© Part 8: Assembly


<InArticleAd />

<BionicText method="advanced" intensity="medium" class="highlight">
Assembly is where your individual parts come together as a functioning mechanism. FreeCAD's Assembly workbench uses constraints to define how parts relate to each other, allowing you to test motion and verify your design before manufacturing.
</BionicText>

### Assembly Strategy

<Card title="üéØ Assembly Constraints Plan" icon="rocket">

1. **Frame:** Fixed (ground link)
2. **Crank:** Rotates about fixed pivot at origin
3. **Connecting Rod:** Pivots on crank pin and slider pin
4. **Slider:** Constrained to linear horizontal motion

</Card>

### Creating the Assembly

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Setup">

    <Steps>

    1. **Switch to Assembly workbench**

       Use workbench dropdown

    2. **Create new assembly**

       Assembly ‚Üí Create Assembly

    3. **Add parts**

       Drag parts from tree or use "Add Part" button:
       - Frame
       - Crank
       - ConnectingRod
       - Slider

    </Steps>

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Fixing Frame">

    <Steps>

    1. Select **Frame** in assembly tree

    2. Click **Fixed constraint** button

    3. Frame is now locked in place (ground link)

    </Steps>

    :::note[Why Fix the Frame?]
    Every mechanism needs a ground reference. The frame provides this fixed reference point from which all other motion is measured.
    :::

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Crank Rotation">

    **Allow crank to rotate:**

    <Steps>

    1. Select **crank center hole axis**

    2. Select **frame pivot hole axis**

    3. Click **Axial Align constraint**

    </Steps>

    This aligns the axes and allows rotation! Crank can now spin about its shaft.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Rod Connections">

    **Connect rod to crank:**

    <Steps>
    1. Rod left hole axis ‚Üí Crank offset pin hole axis
    2. Axial Align constraint
    </Steps>

    **Connect rod to slider:**

    <Steps>
    1. Rod right hole axis ‚Üí Slider pin hole axis
    2. Axial Align constraint
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Slider Motion">

    **Constrain slider to linear motion:**

    <Steps>
    1. Select **slider bottom face**
    2. Select **frame top face**
    3. Apply **Plane coincident** or **Linear constraint**
    </Steps>

    This keeps slider on the guide!

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Testing">

    **Test the mechanism:**

    Try dragging the crank in the assembly view:
    - Crank should rotate
    - Rod should follow
    - Slider should move linearly!

    :::tip[Troubleshooting Motion]
    **If mechanism won't move:**
    - Check that only Frame is Fixed
    - All pin joints should be Axial Align (not Fixed!)
    - Remove any redundant constraints
    - Ensure DOF (degrees of freedom) allows rotation
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## ‚ùì Common Issues and Solutions


<InArticleAd />

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Sketch Issues">

    **"Sketch not fully constrained"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Check Solver Messages: "X degrees of freedom"
    - Add missing constraints until DOF = 0
    - Common missing: Horizontal/Vertical on lines, Coincident on centers
    :::

    **"Cannot pad sketch"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Sketch must form closed regions
    - Check for gaps in your profile
    - Ensure lines connect at endpoints
    :::

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Parametric Issues">

    **"Formula error in dimension"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Check spelling: `Spreadsheet.CrankRadius` (case-sensitive!)
    - Verify alias was created in spreadsheet properties
    - Try re-entering the formula
    :::

    **"Part doesn't update with parameter"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Did you use formula or hardcode value?
    - Check dimension: Should show formula icon (∆íx)
    - Manually recompute that body (right-click ‚Üí Recompute)
    :::

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Assembly Issues">

    **"Parts don't move"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Only Frame should be Fixed!
    - All pins should be Axial Align (not Fixed)
    - Remove redundant constraints
    - Check for over-constraint (too many constraints)
    :::

    **"Parts misaligned"**

    :::caution[Solution]
    - Verify hole radii match between mating parts
    - Check that axes are properly selected
    - Try removing and re-applying constraint
    :::

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
